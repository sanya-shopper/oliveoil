Chapter 03

Prices & Quotes

Olive oil is a weather-driven commodity whose price roughly tripled and then fell back inside two years. Here's what it costs today, where to watch it for free, what a bottle really costs to make, and how to ask for a quote like a pro.

Why the price won't sit still

A severe multi-year Mediterranean drought — Spain alone grows around 40% of the world's olive oil — cut supply hard and pushed bulk extra-virgin to record highs. Global bulk EVOO peaked near US$10,300 per metric ton in January 2024, roughly $10.28/kg; Spanish wholesale touched about €9,000/tonne around the same time 1.

Then the rain returned. The EU's 2024/25 harvest rebounded to roughly 1.9–2.0 million tonnes (Spain up almost 50%), and prices fell about a third from the peak 2. By January 2026, bulk conventional EVOO ran roughly €4.50–5.00/kg from Spain, ~€6.90/kg from Italy, and ~€3.40/kg from Tunisia 3.

And it may rise again In mid-2026 Spanish industry bodies warned that most of the 2025/26 crop had already sold and that early data pointed to a sharply lower 2026/27 flowering — i.e., another price rise is a live risk 3. Don't build a business plan on today's trough.

Where to watch prices — for free

Free & public

  • International Olive Council (IOC) — monthly stats with weekly producer prices (Jaén, Bari, Chania) 4
  • PoolRed — the Andalusian price database that is the Spanish reference
  • USDA FAS GAIN reports — global production, stocks, price context
  • Selina Wamucii — daily producer/export prices by country, incl. the US 5
  • Olive Oil Times, Tridge, EU market observatory — news + trackers

Paid / subscription

  • Mintec — commodity price intelligence & benchmarks
  • Expana — agri-food price benchmarks
  • Tridge Premium — deeper export/supplier data

For a startup, the free sources plus one real supplier quote are plenty. Subscriptions are for when you're buying containers.

How to get a quote

  1. Send a clear RFQ

    State the grade (EVOO / organic / specific origin), volume, packaging (retail glass/tin vs. bulk drum/IBC/flexitank), delivery destination and schedule, required certifications, and your preferred Incoterms.

  2. Request samples before committing

    Reputable suppliers send sample sets (often 3 × 250 ml), free or freight-only, in about 10–14 days. Taste and smell them — and, ideally, lab-verify.

  3. Negotiate terms, then trial-order

    Settle price, MOQ, lead time, and payment. Place a small trial order before signing any supply contract.

What to expect

  • MOQ: small distributors sell by the case or a few gallons; true bulk starts near 20 t = one flexitank; programs run 100+ t/yr with monthly draws.
  • Incoterms: EXW, FOB, CIF, or DDP — DDP (delivered, duty paid) is simplest for a first-time buyer but priciest.
  • Lead times: samples ~10–14 days; a container from the Mediterranean often 4–10+ weeks door-to-door.
  • Payment: often a deposit + balance (e.g. 30% down / 70% before shipment); net terms only after a relationship exists.

Who to ask

  • Domestic mills for US-origin oil (see Sourcing): Desert Milling, Sutter Buttes, Wild Groves, Sonoma Farm, Corto, Cobram/COR.
  • Importers & bulk brokers for imported or blended oil: Jedwards / Bulk Natural Oils, Olive Oil Lovers, Centra Foods.

Specs to demand on a Certificate of Analysis

Quality is invisible in the bottle — insist on the paperwork 8:

  • Harvest / crush date and best-by (freshness is everything)
  • Free fatty acid / free acidity — extra-virgin must be ≤ 0.8%; lower is better
  • Peroxide value — EVOO ≤ 20 meq O₂/kg; lower means fresher
  • UV absorbance (K232 / K270) — oxidation indicators
  • Total polyphenols (mg/kg) — a quality and health-marketing signal
  • Fatty-acid & sterol profile — the authenticity / adulteration check
  • Certifications — USDA NOP / EU Organic, Kosher, Halal, non-GMO, any PDO/PGI
